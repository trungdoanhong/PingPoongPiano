Piano Music Theory Guide

Basic Music Notation

Music is written on a staff, which consists of five lines and four spaces. Notes are placed on the lines and spaces to indicate their pitch. The higher a note appears on the staff, the higher its pitch.

Notes and Pitch

The musical alphabet consists of seven notes: A, B, C, D, E, F, and G. These notes repeat in a cycle as you move up or down in pitch. On a piano, the white keys represent these natural notes.

Rhythm and Time

Rhythm is the pattern of time in music. It's organized into measures, which are divided by bar lines. Each measure contains a specific number of beats, as determined by the time signature.

Time Signatures

The time signature appears at the beginning of a piece and consists of two numbers. The top number indicates how many beats are in each measure, while the bottom number indicates what type of note gets one beat.

Common time signatures include:

  • 4/4: Four quarter notes per measure (most common)
  • 3/4: Three quarter notes per measure (waltz time)
  • 2/4: Two quarter notes per measure (march time)
  • 6/8: Six eighth notes per measure (often felt as two groups of three)

Scales and Keys

A scale is a sequence of notes arranged in ascending or descending order. The most common scales in Western music are the major and minor scales.

Major Scale

The major scale has a bright, happy sound. The pattern of whole steps (W) and half steps (H) in a major scale is: W-W-H-W-W-W-H.

For example, the C Major scale consists of the notes: C, D, E, F, G, A, B, C.

Minor Scale

The natural minor scale has a darker, more melancholic sound. The pattern of whole steps and half steps is: W-H-W-W-H-W-W.

For example, the A Minor scale consists of the notes: A, B, C, D, E, F, G, A.

Key Signatures

A key signature appears at the beginning of a piece and indicates which notes should be consistently played as sharps or flats throughout the piece. It establishes the key of the music.

Chords

A chord is a group of three or more notes played simultaneously. The most common chords are triads, which consist of three notes built on intervals of thirds.

Major Triad

A major triad consists of a root note, a major third (4 half steps above the root), and a perfect fifth (7 half steps above the root).

For example, a C Major triad consists of the notes C, E, and G.

Minor Triad

A minor triad consists of a root note, a minor third (3 half steps above the root), and a perfect fifth (7 half steps above the root).

For example, an A Minor triad consists of the notes A, C, and E.

Chord Progressions

A chord progression is a sequence of chords played in succession. Common chord progressions in popular music include:

  • I-IV-V: The foundation of countless songs
  • I-V-vi-IV: Used in many pop songs
  • ii-V-I: Common in jazz music

Practice Tips

  • Start slow: Begin practicing at a tempo where you can play accurately, then gradually increase speed.
  • Use a metronome: This helps develop a consistent sense of timing.
  • Practice regularly: Consistent, daily practice is more effective than occasional long sessions.
  • Learn scales: They build finger strength and dexterity while teaching you about keys.
  • Play by ear: Try to figure out simple melodies without sheet music to develop your musical ear.
  • Record yourself: This helps you hear mistakes and track your progress.
